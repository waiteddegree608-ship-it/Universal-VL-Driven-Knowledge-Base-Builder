**Buying and Selling**  

**Introduction.** In previous chapters, we studied the behavior of consumers who start out without owning any goods, but who had some money with which to buy goods. In this chapter, the consumer has an *initial endowment*, which is the bundle of goods the consumer owns before any trades are made. A consumer can trade away from his initial endowment by selling one good and buying the other.  

The techniques that you have already learned will serve you well here. To find out how much a consumer demands at given prices, you find his budget line and then find a point of tangency between his budget line and an indifference curve. To determine a budget line for a consumer who is trading from an initial endowment and who has no source of income other than his initial endowment, notice two things. First, *the initial endowment must lie on the consumer’s budget line*. This is true because, no matter what the prices are, the consumer can always afford his initial endowment. Second, *if the prices are \( p_1 \) and \( p_2 \), the slope of the budget line must be \( -p_1/p_2 \)*. This is true, since for every unit of good 1 the consumer gives up, he can get exactly \( p_1/p_2 \) units of good 2. Therefore if you know the prices and you know the consumer’s initial endowment, then you can always write an equation for the consumer’s budget line. After all, if you know one point on a line and you know its slope, you can either draw the line or write down its equation. Once you have the budget equation, you can find the bundle the consumer chooses, using the same methods you learned in Chapter 5.  

**Example:** A peasant consumes only rice and fish. He grows some rice and some fish, but not necessarily in the same proportion in which he wants to consume them. Suppose that if he makes no trades, he will have 20 units of rice and 5 units of fish. The price of rice is 1 yuan per unit, and the price of fish is 2 yuan per unit. The value of the peasant’s endowment is \( (1 \times 20) + (2 \times 5) = 30 \). Therefore the peasant can consume any bundle \( (R, F) \) such that \( (1 \times R) + (2 \times F) = 30 \).  

Perhaps the most interesting application of trading from an initial endowment is the theory of labor supply. To study labor supply, we consider the behavior of a consumer who is choosing between leisure and other goods. The only thing that is at all new or “tricky” is finding the appropriate budget constraint for the problem at hand. To study labor supply, we think of the consumer as having an initial endowment of leisure, some of which he may trade away for goods.  

In most applications we set the price of “other goods” at 1. The wage rate is the price of leisure. The role that is played by income in the ordinary consumer-good model is now played by “full income.” A worker’s full income is the income she would have if she chose to take no leisure.  

**Formulas:**  
- \( (1 \times 20) + (2 \times 5) = 30 \)  
- Budget constraint: \( (1 \times R) + (2 \times F) = 30 \)  
- Budget line slope: \( -p_1/p_2 \)

## Detailed Analysis  
This chapter extends the consumer choice framework from **Chapter 5** (covering budget lines and indifference curve tangency) to cases where consumers start with an initial endowment of goods, rather than fixed monetary income. Key insights and their microeconomic implications are synthesized below without restating introductory text.  

### Core Concepts and Continuity with Prior Knowledge  
- **Initial endowment as the foundation**:  
  Unlike earlier chapters where consumers begin with monetary income, the budget line here *must pass through the initial endowment point* because it represents a feasible bundle (no trade). This preserves the Chapter 5 principle that budget constraints define affordable choices, but reframes "income" as the market value of endowments. The consumer’s problem reduces to maximizing utility subject to \( p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = p_1 \omega_1 + p_2 \omega_2 \), where \( (\omega_1, \omega_2) \) is the initial endowment. The slope \( -p_1/p_2 \) mirrors Chapter 5, reflecting the marginal rate of transformation (opportunity cost) in trade.  

- **Budget line derivation**:  
  The example with rice (\( R \)) and fish (\( F \)) demonstrates how to convert endowments to a single budget constraint: endowment value (\( 30 \)) serves as "full income." Critically, a change in prices (e.g., fish price rises) rotates the budget line around the endowment point, unlike fixed-income models where price changes shift the line parallel. This rotation introduces **endowment income effects**, which are central to analyzing real-world trading behavior (e.g., farmers adjusting consumption to market prices).  

### Critical Application: Labor Supply Theory  
The labor-leisure model is a pivotal extension:  
- **Leisure as an initial endowment**: Total time \( T \) (e.g., 24 hours) is the endowment. If \( L \) = leisure consumed and \( C \) = consumption of other goods, the budget constraint becomes \( C = w(T - L) \), where \( w \) is the wage rate. Setting the price of "other goods" to 1 simplifies this to \( C + wL = wT \), making \( wT \) the **full income** (income if no leisure is taken).  
- **Key implication for choice**: The consumer trades leisure for goods at a price \( w \), so utility maximization now involves tangency between the \( (C, L) \) indifference curve and the budget line. This reframing reveals why wage changes may reduce labor supply (backward-bending labor supply curve) due to income versus substitution effects—extending Chapter 5’s demand analysis to time allocation.  

### Why This Framework Matters  
The endowment model clarifies **Pareto efficiency** in exchanges (later in Chapter 31) and resolves the "giving up goods to gain goods" paradox: Trade occurs because endowment proportions rarely match preferences. For instance, the fish-rice peasant will sell surplus goods to reach an optimal bundle, illustrating how relative prices drive mutually beneficial transactions. This underpins market equilibrium analysis without assuming monetary income, providing a more realistic basis for policy evaluation (e.g., welfare impacts of price controls on producers).  

*Continuity note: The tangency condition from Chapter 5 (MRS = \( p_1/p_2 \)) holds here, but the endowment point anchors the budget constraint, altering how price changes affect demand. No prior content is repeated; instead, this synthesizes endowment-based choice as the natural progression from fixed-income models.*

### 2. 关键经济含义解析  
#### **禀赋模型下价格变动的差异机制**  
- **9.1 (d) 的预算线移动**：  
  Abishag 先出售 10 夸脱（从禀赋点 \( (20,5) \) 移动至束 C），后柑橘价格下降至与夸脱相等（\( p_K = p_Q \)）。  
  - **预算线变化**：价格比例改变（从 \( p_Q/p_K = 1/4 \) 变为 1），但移动起点非原始禀赋点，而是交易后的束 C。  
  - **经济含义**：禀赋收入效应仅部分吸收价格变动——出售夸脱后，其“初始”禀赋已转移，新预算线斜率变化但截距取决于 C 点的现金余额。这验证上文 **“禀赋点锚定预算约束”** 机制：价格变动影响需求路径，但因交易改变参考点，变动效应弱于完全静态禀赋模型。  

- **9.2 (c) vs. 9.2 (d) 的对比**：  
  - **9.2 (c)**（价格变动前未交易）：  
    番茄价格上升，禀赋价值从 $200 增至 $300。预算线平行外移（因 \( \frac{\Delta \text{value}}{p_E} = 300/5 = 60 \) 横轴截距，\( \frac{300}{15} = 20 \) 纵轴截距），斜率变陡（从 \( -1 \) 至 \( -1/3 \)）。  
    → **核心机制**：禀赋价值同比例变化吸收价格冲击（茄子价格不变，番茄价格涨 200%，禀赋价值涨 50%），但因秉赋失衡（30E:10T），实际购买力提升（新消费束 15T:15E 达更高无差异曲线）。这体现 **“价格变动通过禀赋收入效应改变可行集”**，与固定收入模型根本差异。  
  - **9.2 (d)**（价格变动前已变现）：  
    现金收入 $200 固定，番茄涨价后预算线旋转（斜率从 \( -1 \) 变为 \( -1/3 \)），截距均减小（\( T_{\max} = 200/15 \approx 13.3 \), \( E_{\max} = 40 \)）。  
    → **与上文关键区别**：此情境退化为固定收入模型（无禀赋锚定），价格变动导致纯替代效应（消费束降至 10T:10E）。这强化上文 **“禀赋模型提供更现实的政策评估基础”**——如价格管制对生产者的影响，需区分禀赋持有者（9.2c）与纯消费者（9.2d）行为。  

#### **显示偏好与交易福利的微观基础**  
- **9.1 (e) 的不选择区域**：  
  红预算线中，波浪线部分（通常为高于原选择束 C 的区域）因违反弱显示偏好公理被排除。  
  - **动态含义**：真实交易中，消费者行为受历史交易路径约束。这呼应上文 **“价格驱动互惠交易”** ——禀赋基础模型能预测交易序列的福利变化（如出售夸脱后降低柑橘价格，是否提升效用），而固定收入模型无法追踪此类路径依赖。  

**连续性总结**：  
本例深化上文从固定收入到禀赋模型的跃迁。当禀赋未变现时（9.2c），价格变动通过收入效应主导需求；一旦变现（9.2d），则降级为传统模型。这为政策分析提供精准框架：例如，价格支持政策若忽略生产者禀赋，将误判福利影响（9.2c 中生产者实际受益，9.2d 中纯消费者受损）。同时，9.1 (e) 证明禀赋交易路径完整性是揭示真实偏好的必要条件，使市场均衡分析免于“货币收入”失真假设。
